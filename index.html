About

Built in Alaska. Shaped by It.

I grew up in Alaska and never really left — and I never wanted to. The state shaped everything about how I see the world: the scale, the isolation, the resilience, and the way communities here depend on infrastructure that most people never think about. When that infrastructure fails, it's not an inconvenience — it's a crisis. That reality has defined my career.

My path here wasn't linear. I started as a small business owner, then moved into marketing at GCI, Alaska's largest telecommunications provider, before pivoting into cybersecurity and IT. Over two decades, I've worked my way into the role of Director of Security Architecture, where I oversee security project management, strategic planning, and security architecture for the company. I've had a front-row seat to how Alaska's telecom infrastructure is simultaneously critical, vulnerable, and underappreciated.

That's what drove me back to school. I'm currently a Ph.D. student in the Interdisciplinary Ph.D. program at the University of Alaska Fairbanks, researching the cyber and physical security of Alaska's telecommunications critical infrastructure in an Arctic geopolitical context. I saw a gap between what practitioners like me experience on the ground and what the academic and policy literature actually addresses — and I decided to be the one to close it.

Outside of work and research, I'm a two-time cancer survivor, a completed three half marathons, and a proud family man. I believe that cybersecurity doesn't have to be impenetrable jargon — it can and should be accessible to everyone whose life depends on the systems we protect. And in Alaska, that's everyone.

Research

Dissertation Research

"Maintaining Security of Critical Telecommunications Infrastructure in Alaska: Cyber and Physical Risk and Resilience in a Strategic Arctic Context"

Alaska's telecommunications critical infrastructure faces escalating security risk driven by its strategic importance to national defense, aging physical assets, remote and inaccessible geographies, and the intensifying competition among great powers in the Arctic. This research investigates the nature, scope, and perception of those risks — and the organizational and structural barriers that stand in the way of addressing them.

Institution University of Alaska Fairbanks — Interdisciplinary Ph.D.
Committee Chair Dr. Arghya Das
Expected Completion Spring 2027

Research Questions

01

What internal, external, and natural threats confront Alaska's telecommunications infrastructure, and in what ways do these threats manifest as cyber and physical security risks?

02

What organizational, technical, regulatory, and geographic barriers limit the mitigation of these risks?

03

How are these risks perceived and prioritized by telecom security and risk leaders in Alaska?

Methodology

Literature Review

200+ sources spanning cybersecurity, Arctic geopolitics, critical infrastructure protection, and organizational resilience. Comparative analysis with Nordic nations, especially Finland.

Industry Survey

Anonymous online survey targeting Alaska telecom security and risk leaders — evaluating risk tradeoff decisions.

Semi-Structured Interviews

In-depth interviews with selected survey respondents, capturing richer narrative context around risk decision-making, organizational constraints, and threat prioritization.

Why This Research Matters

Alaska sits at the intersection of great power competition, accelerating climate change, and a critical dependency on telecommunications infrastructure that cannot afford to fail. Recent events make this research urgent:

2018

Matanuska-Susitna Borough declared a disaster following a devastating cyberattack that crippled borough systems for months.

2023

Chinese "Gate Crashers" were intercepted at Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson, underscoring intelligence threats on Alaskan soil.

2024

Russian vessels engaged in cable-dragging incidents in the Baltic Sea, demonstrating the vulnerability of undersea telecommunications infrastructure.

2025

Chinese state-sponsored groups infiltrated major U.S. telecommunications providers, compromising sensitive communications infrastructure.

2026

Russia and China deepened Arctic cooperation agreements, formalizing coordination in a region where Alaska's infrastructure and security interests are directly at stake.

This is not an abstract academic exercise. Alaska's telecommunications infrastructure is a strategic asset — and the people who protect it need research that reflects the reality they face.
