See You at Alaska Cyber Group — and Help Shape Arctic Telecom Security Research

Why I'll be at the Alaska Cyber Group Annual Conference — and how practitioners across the state can help ground this research in reality.

Mitch Kitter Director, Security Architecture at GCI · Ph.D. Student, UAF

Tomorrow I'll be at the Alaska Cyber Group Annual Conference at the UAA Student Union Building — and I'm genuinely excited about it. Not just because it's one of the best rooms of security practitioners in the state, but because it lands at exactly the right moment in my research.

I'm deep into my Ph.D. dissertation at UAF, studying the cyber and physical security of Alaska's telecommunications critical infrastructure. The core question I keep coming back to is simple to ask and hard to answer: what keeps Alaska's telecom security leaders up at night, and why is it so difficult to do something about it?

The Alaska Cyber Group conference is exactly the kind of place where those answers live — in the hallways, over coffee, in the frank conversations that happen when practitioners aren't performing for a PowerPoint. I'm particularly looking forward to the panel discussion, Preparing for What's Next — Perspectives on Risk, Resilience, and Change. That framing — risk, resilience, and change — maps almost directly onto the questions I'm trying to answer. The tension between knowing what the risks are and actually being resourced and organized to address them is real, and I expect that panel to surface some of it honestly.

A research booth, and a survey built for Alaska's telecom leaders

I'll have a research booth at the event where I'm collecting responses to my IRB-approved survey — a quick, anonymous instrument designed specifically for Alaska telecom security and risk leaders. It uses a Best-Worst Scaling format to understand how practitioners prioritize competing risks when resources are finite and the threat landscape is complicated. If you're a CISO, CTO, CIO, or fill a similar role at an Alaska telecom provider, your perspective is exactly what this research needs. The population is small, the stakes are real, and your voice matters.

Beyond the survey, I'm just looking forward to the conversations. I want to hear from practitioners across Alaska's critical infrastructure — not just telecom — about the threats, vulnerabilities, and organizational friction they're navigating. That practitioner knowledge is the kind of grounding that makes research useful rather than academic.

Come find me at the booth. I'd love to talk.
